You are building in a category where almost every competitor has an answer to the "we use AI" question, and most of those answers fall apart the moment a HIPAA officer or a Medicaid MCO asks a real follow-up. That is the gap this page is about.

Home-care apps tend to lose time in three places. Shift-note documentation that eats 10 to 15 minutes per visit and still produces text too messy for downstream use. EVV compliance integrations that multiply every time a new state aggregator changes its schema. Caregiver-to-family matching that runs on thin heuristics and produces no-show rates the team feels but cannot quite diagnose. None of these are single-layer problems. Each has a small slice that genuinely belongs to AI, a larger slice that belongs to rule-based logic, and a much larger slice that is traditional code and database work. Mixing them up is expensive.

Here is the frame we use. Roughly 60% of any product problem lives at the traditional code and database layer. Around 30% is rule-based logic that should be explicit, versioned, and auditable. The remaining 10% is a genuine AI problem, and that 10% is where a thoughtful orchestration pays for itself. For a home-care app specifically, the AI slice is usually structured extraction inside shift notes, match-quality scoring where human signal is thin, and a narrow set of safety flags on care-plan drift. Almost everything else is better served by a query, a rule, or a form field.

We did a scoped sprint with Feeld, a product company with a CTO who was in the same shape: a technical team with the right instincts and a stack that had quietly accumulated too many LLM calls in places where Postgres would have done the job faster, cheaper, and with better guarantees. The deliverables were a workshop, an Organizational Context Architecture, and a Strategic Operations Framework that gave their team a durable way to answer "where does this feature belong." That is the same thing Homecare Application needs before the next EVV state goes live.

On the governance side, the relevant paper is the Ethics Engine, a psychometric assessment tool for evaluating ideological and moral patterns in LLMs, with the preprint at arxiv.org/abs/2510.11742 and the repo at github.com/RinDig/AuditEngine. It gives a technical team a defensible answer when a HIPAA officer or an MCO compliance lead asks how the model is being audited.

Where the work tends to belong

  1. Traditional code

    Scheduling, shift state, payroll exports, audit logs, data model around caregivers, clients, and visits.

  2. Rule-based logic

    EVV per-state rules. Overtime and break thresholds. Care-plan compliance checks. Escalation policies.

  3. Genuine AI

    Structured extraction inside free-text shift notes. Match-quality scoring. Narrow safety flags on care-plan drift.

Three pains that show up in almost every home-care app

Documentation

Shift notes eat 10 to 15 minutes a visit and still produce messy text.

Most teams have tried an LLM auto-fill, hit a HIPAA wall, and shelved it. The useful cut is the small set of structured fields the AI actually needs to touch.

Compliance

Every new EVV state means another one-off integration.

One adapter with rule-based state overrides beats five to eight bespoke integrations and dreading the next state aggregator schema change.

Marketplace

Matching runs on heuristics and no-shows spike in the first two weeks.

Match quality is almost never the LLM problem founders think it is. It is a structured-feature problem with a thin AI layer on top, and wrong-layer choices have usually already burned months.
